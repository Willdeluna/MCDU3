# Making RFMC navigation displays feel like the real 737 and A320

## How the A320 side really works

Public A320-family material from urlAirbushttps://www.airbus.com/en shows an EFIS/ECAM architecture built around six identical 7.25-by-7.25 inch CRT display units, with three Display Management Computers generating the images and feeding the PFD, ND, and ECAM displays digitally. In that architecture, the ND is not a “smart map widget” sitting by itself; it is one visual endpoint in a display-management chain that takes inputs from ADIRS, FMGC, FACs, FCU, radios, weather radar, and warning computers. That matters for realism, because it means you should model the A320 ND as rendered symbology driven by upstream systems, not as a free-standing panel with its own ad hoc settings. citeturn39view0

The same Airbus material is very clear about the core A320 ND geometry. `ROSE` mode places the aircraft symbol in the screen centre and is heading-up; `ARC` puts the aircraft symbol in the lower part of the screen and is also heading-up; `PLAN` centres the display on the selected waypoint and is north-up. It also states that in `ROSE-NAV`, `ARC`, and `PLAN`, map data from the FMS are presented. That single paragraph is probably the most important visual rule for your renderer: on the A320 side, mode selection changes projection, centring, and ownership of map content all at once. citeturn39view0turn22view0turn22view2turn23view4

The A320 FMGS split is equally important. Airbus describes the two MCDUs as the long-term crew interface to the FMGCs for flight plan definition, data entry, and functions such as direct-to, offset, and secondary flight plan selection. The FCU is the short-term interface. The NDs are the visual interface that present flight plan, aircraft position and path, and navigation items such as radio navaids and wind. In normal dual mode, both FMGCs compute independently, one is master and the other slave, and both MCDUs act independently while entries are automatically recopied and applied to both FMGCs; on failure, cross-side use is supported. citeturn29view1turn43view0

That means your app should not treat the A320 ND as a page of the MCDU. The MCDU owns data entry and revision. The EFIS side owns display mode and range. The ND then renders the resulting active route, predictions, tuned navaid cues, and map overlays. Airbus’s radio-management description reinforces this: navigation tuning can be automatic under FMGS control, manual through the FMGS CDU without breaking automatic behaviour, or back-up tuning through the Radio Management Panel when both FMGCs are lost; if one FMGC fails, the remaining one controls all receivers. citeturn29view2turn43view0

The more modern Pegasus FMS material from urlHoneywell Aerospacehttps://aerospace.honeywell.com is especially useful because it describes A320-series ND-visible behaviour that most hobby implementations skip. It notes that pseudo-waypoints such as `CONF1` and `CONF2` can be displayed on both MCDU and ND, that DIRTO improvements keep the real TO waypoint in the ND upper-right rather than showing an abeam pseudo-waypoint there, that DIRTO temporary vectors can be displayed on both NDs, and that later software expands the number of down-path transitions shown on the ND. Those are exactly the kinds of small state-dependent details that make a display feel authentic. citeturn42view0

## How the 737 side really works

On the 737 side, material based on urlBoeinghttps://www.boeing.com flight-instrument documentation describes a different display personality. The Common Display System supplies information on six flat-panel liquid-crystal display units, not CRTs, and the system uses ambient-light sensors plus pilot brightness controls to manage display intensity. That is a strong cue for your rendering layer: the 737 NG should look flatter, sharper, and more LCD-like than the A320 side, with far less phosphor-style bloom. citeturn38view2

The 737 EFIS control panel is also much more explicitly documented in public material. The mode selector gives `APP`, `VOR`, `MAP`, and `PLN`. `APP` is heading-up and shows localiser/glideslope or IAN-style approach information. `VOR` is heading-up and shows reference VOR data. `MAP` shows FMC-generated route and map information in track-up format, including waypoints within the selected range and VNAV path deviation. `PLN` is a non-moving, true-north-up route depiction. The `CTR` pushbutton toggles centred versus expanded presentations for the applicable modes, and the range selector changes nautical-mile scale. citeturn25view0

The overlay logic is another place where realism lives. The 737 panel has dedicated controls for `TFC`, plus map-option switches for `WXR`, `STA`, `WPT`, `ARPT`, `DATA`, `POS`, and `TERR`. Those are not generic “show me more stuff” toggles. They are explicitly limited by mode and, in some cases, by range. `STA` shows all database navaids only on 5, 10, 20, or 40 nm scales and high-altitude navaids on 80 through 640 nm scales. `WPT` shows non-flight-plan waypoints only if the selected range is 40 nm or less. `ARPT` shows airports in the viewable map area. `DATA` adds waypoint constraints and ETA data. `POS` adds IRS/GPS/VOR position cues. `TERR` is only available in certain modes. That sort of range-gated eligibility is exactly what gives real displays their disciplined, non-game-like feel. citeturn26view0

The FMC relationship is also more nuanced than “CDU drives map”. In dual-FMC normal mode, the left FMC is primary, the right FMC operates in synchronisation with it, and the maps display composite information from both FMCs. Moving the source select switch to `BOTH ON L` or `BOTH ON R` isolates operation to one side, changes the opposite-map annunciation to `FMC L` or `FMC R`, and can disengage LNAV/VNAV. If you want the 737 side of your app to feel correct, those source annuncs and side relationships matter. citeturn36view1turn36view2turn36view3turn36view4

The 737 documentation also gives you a very clear model for how CDU edits should affect the ND. In `PLN`, the route can be stepped using the CDU `LEGS` page, and the waypoint at the centre of the display is identified as `CTR` on the CDU `RTE LEGS` page. On the CDU itself, selecting procedures or route options creates a route modification marked `<SEL>`; once the modification is executed it becomes `<ACT>`. Direct-to makes the selected waypoint active, and holding-pattern creation inserts a `HOLD AT` waypoint into the route sequence only after execution. That is a strong hint that your 737 ND should support a visible distinction between active route and modified-but-not-yet-executed route state. citeturn38view1turn27view0turn27view4turn27view5

One more useful detail for later fidelity work: Boeing documentation says the crew must manually tune ILS and VOR receivers for raw display on the DUs, while FMC messages also exist for cases where the FMC is unable to auto-tune or receive a required navaid for certain guided procedures. So if you later add raw-data or approach-source logic, the ND should be able to reflect both manually tuned radio state and FMC-driven procedure state rather than collapsing them into one generic source flag. citeturn40view1turn41view0

## What the FMC should change on the ND

The single most important modelling rule is this: the ND is not another FMC page. On both families, the route computer and the display controls have separate jobs. The FMC or FMGS owns route structure, direct-to logic, holds, constraints, predictions, and radio-nav context. The EFIS side owns display mode, range, centring, traffic/weather/terrain layers, and optional map overlays. The display itself is the rendered result of both. If you encode that split in software, the UI will start feeling real almost immediately. citeturn29view1turn25view0turn26view0turn38view1

- Loading a route or selecting a departure, arrival, or approach should change route geometry, active waypoint, waypoint predictions, and any displayed constraints. On the A320 side that means flight-plan presentation, position/path, radio items, and upper-right waypoint data; on the 737 side it means active-route geometry, active waypoint information, and, if `DATA` is selected, waypoint constraint and ETA overlays. citeturn23view2turn23view3turn29view1turn26view0

- A non-executed route change should not instantly overwrite the active map. On the 737 side, Boeing explicitly distinguishes `<SEL>` modifications from `<ACT>` routes until `EXEC`. On the A320 side, Honeywell documents temporary DIRTO vectors and active/secondary plan behaviour that are visible on the ND. Your renderer therefore needs at least two route layers: active geometry and temporary geometry. citeturn27view0turn42view0

- `DIR TO` and `HOLD` are not cosmetic toggles. They create new geometry and should change what the pilot sees. Boeing says direct-to makes the selected waypoint active and hold insertion changes route sequence after execution; Honeywell says improved DIRTO handling affects what appears in the A320 ND upper-right waypoint block and whether temporary vectors appear on one or both NDs. citeturn27view4turn27view5turn42view0

- Mode changes must change the map transform, not just the label. On the A320 side, `ROSE`, `ARC`, and `PLAN` alter centring and orientation. On the 737 side, `APP`, `VOR`, `MAP`, and `PLN`, plus `CTR`, alter format, expansion, and orientation. If a mode change does not move the aircraft symbol, change north-versus-heading logic, or re-centre around a waypoint when appropriate, it will feel wrong even if the colours are right. citeturn39view0turn25view0turn38view1

- Overlay switches should only affect eligible symbol classes, and on the 737 side they should obey documented range gates. That means “button pressed” is not enough as a state model; you also need “was this symbol allowed in this mode and at this scale?” as part of the rendering pipeline. citeturn26view0

## Why the current ND still reads like a web app

The first big reason is that the current rendering appears to use a single generic visual language for both families, but the real hardware is not generic. The A320 baseline public material describes CRT display units and DMC-generated imagery, while the 737 NG CDS documentation describes flat-panel LCD units with ambient-light-driven brightness control. If you make both displays share one skin, one glow model, and one typography style, they will immediately feel less authentic than they should. citeturn39view0turn38view2

The second reason is screen density and information hierarchy. In your screenshot, the left display shows one mode label, one track readout, one range value, a large `NO ROUTE`, and a simplified set of arcs. Real displays are much denser. The A320 references show speed, wind, top-right waypoint/bearing/distance/ETA, lower-corner navaid information, and mode-specific annotations. The 737 MAP examples show distance-to-waypoint, TAS, heading box, source annunciation, overlay annunciations, ETA, groundspeed, magnetic/true reference, active route, vertical deviation, and position-difference cues. You do not need to reproduce every possible label on day one, but you do need to reproduce the idea that these are data-dense avionics displays rather than sparse teaching diagrams. citeturn29view0turn38view0

The third reason is semantic colour. The A320 family material explicitly maps colour to meaning: red for warnings, amber for cautions, green for normal indications, white for titles and guiding remarks, blue for actions, and magenta for particular messages. Your current palette is attractive, but it still feels like a designer-picked neon palette rather than a meaning-first avionics palette. A much more convincing approach is to define colour tokens by operational meaning and let each display family map those tokens differently. citeturn39view0

The fourth reason is geometry. Your current A320-facing screen shows a generic arc map with big rectangular touch controls attached directly to it. The real units separate the image from the control panel. Even if you keep touch controls for training, the simulated display itself should behave like the instrument first and the training UI second. The moment you move family-correct mode/range/filter controls into a detachable “training rail” and leave the screen face to carry only true in-display legends, source blocks, and symbology, the whole thing will feel more believable. Your own repo already supports touch targets and tutorial overlays, so you can make that change without sacrificing usability. citeturn44view1turn25view0turn26view0

A very specific mismatch in your current A320-facing view is naming. `MAP` belongs to the 737’s documented mode set, while the A320 family public references call the core display modes `ROSE`, `ARC`, and `PLAN`. So the first easy realism win is simply to stop using a Boeing mode name on an A320-style display. Likewise, your current `FIX` and `HOLD` controls belong conceptually to FMC functions in your own project rather than to the native ND/EFIS control vocabulary. citeturn39view0turn25view0turn46view0

Finally, the current screen looks too clean. The 737 side should be crisper and flatter, but still not like a modern web dashboard; the A320 side should have slightly more CRT-like bloom, mask softness, edge fall-off, and instrument-mask character. On the 737 side, it is also worth implementing declutter and disagreement logic rather than “always show everything”: Boeing documentation includes amber advisories such as `EXCESS DATA` and range-disagreement messages when the system cannot present the requested amount or combination of map information cleanly. citeturn38view1turn38view2

## A repo-friendly implementation shape

The cleanest fit for your current codebase is to add ND as a sibling subsystem to CDU rather than as an extension of the CDU renderer. The repo already has shared types and FMC logic in `shared/src`, React UI in `src`, and a Zustand-based standalone state model plus a WebSocket-backed connected mode. That is exactly the shape you want for an ND pipeline: route/performance/nav state stays shared, and each ND component becomes a pure renderer of derived state. citeturn44view1

I would split the implementation into three layers. The first layer is a family-neutral semantic state object: mode, range, display-centre policy, orientation, active route geometry, temporary route geometry, waypoint predictions, selected radio sources, overlay selections, and alert/degrade states. The second layer is a family-specific adapter, for example one for the A320 side and one for the 737 side, that translates FMC/FMGS/CDU state plus EFIS settings into those semantics. The third layer is the actual renderer, ideally SVG or canvas, with separate groups for bezel/mask, map space, and annotation space. That lets you keep your avionics geometry deterministic instead of building it out of HTML boxes that never quite line up.

The most important extra state to add is a proper EFIS model separate from FMC state. For the 737 side, that means side-specific EFIS panel state such as mode, centred-versus-expanded, range, VOR/ADF switches, and map-option switches. For the A320 side, it means mode/range and filter selections plus side-specific navaid-display state. In the current screenshot those concerns are fused into one generic bottom strip. In the real aircraft they are separate from the FMC/CDU, and your software should mirror that separation.

I would also give the ND pipeline explicit support for side ownership. The A320 side should know whether it is captain or first officer and which FMGC/radios are feeding it; the 737 side should know whether it is left or right, which FMC source is primary, and what annunciation should appear if source selection changes. That is how you unlock details like `FMC L` and `FMC R` source labels on the 737, or on-side versus off-side MCDU/FMGC behaviour on the A320 side. citeturn36view1turn36view4turn29view1turn43view0

Because your project already has guided tutorials, there is an unusually good opportunity to make the ND not just realistic but explanatory. Every visible symbol should be traceable to a reason: “shown because `WPT` is selected and range is 40 nm or less”, “shown because this is the active TO waypoint”, “shown because PLAN mode centres on the selected waypoint”, “shown in temporary colour because route modification exists but is not executed”. If you add a small optional inspector that exposes those reasons, the ND becomes one of the most educational parts of the app rather than just another pretty panel. citeturn44view0turn44view1turn26view0turn38view1

## The build order with the best payoff

The highest-payoff first step is to separate FMC state from EFIS state and stop using hybrid control labels. Ship an A320-specific mode model with `ROSE`, `ARC`, and `PLAN`, and a 737-specific mode model with `APP`, `VOR`, `MAP`, `PLN`, and `CTR`. That one change will immediately make the display behaviour more truthful and will stop teaching the wrong mental model. citeturn39view0turn25view0

The next step is to add the “always visible” anchor zones that make real displays feel alive even before you draw fancy map geometry: top-left speed and wind blocks, top-right waypoint/bearing/distance/ETA, lower-corner navaid/source blocks, and family-correct source annunciations. Right now the current ND is too empty. These anchor zones are cheap to implement and visually transformative. citeturn29view0turn38view0turn36view0

After that, add route-state fidelity. Support active versus temporary route layers, direct-to preview and commit behaviour, hold insertion after execution, and PLAN-mode step-through. This is where your existing FMC work begins to pay off, because the ND will finally start showing why entries on `LEGS`, `DIR INTC`, `DEP/ARR`, `SEC F-PLN`, or equivalent pages matter. citeturn27view0turn27view4turn27view5turn42view0

Then add overlay discipline. On the 737 side, implement documented range gates and mode limitations for `STA`, `WPT`, `ARPT`, `DATA`, `POS`, `TERR`, `WXR`, and `TFC`. On the A320 side, move towards family-correct ND filter behaviour and in-display legends based on your reference image, rather than treating overlays as generic bottom buttons. This is the stage where the display stops feeling like a toy because it starts refusing to show things that the real display would not show. citeturn26view0turn39view0

Only after the logic feels right would I spend time on the final polish layer: aircraft-specific typography, stroke weights, glow, mask shape, brightness controls, and subtle CRT-versus-LCD treatment. Those details matter a great deal, but they matter most once the map is already being driven by the right state model. The real breakthrough in your app will come from making the ND a rendered consequence of FMC/FMGS plus EFIS state, not from adding more decorative pixels. citeturn39view0turn38view2turn44view1
